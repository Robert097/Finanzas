Income Statement

The income statement, also known as the profit and loss statement (P&L), shows a company's revenues, costs, and expenses during a specific period, ultimately showing the company's net profit or loss.

Key Components

Formula: Net Income = Revenues - Expenses
Component Description Example
Revenues Total income from sales and services $500,000
Cost of Goods Sold Direct costs to produce goods/services $200,000
Gross Profit Revenues - COGS $300,000
Operating Expenses Salaries, rent, utilities, etc. $150,000
Operating Income Gross Profit - Operating Expenses $150,000
Net Income Final profit after all expenses and taxes $120,000

How to Analyze

Gross Profit Margin: (Gross Profit / Revenues) × 100

Operating Profit Margin: (Operating Income / Revenues) × 100

Net Profit Margin: (Net Income / Revenues) × 100

Balance Sheet

The balance sheet provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It shows what the company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities), as well as the amount invested by shareholders (equity).

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
Item Definition Example
Current Assets Cash and assets expected to be converted to cash within one year Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory
Fixed Assets Long-term assets used in business operations Property, Equipment, Buildings
Current Liabilities Debts due to be paid within one year Accounts Payable, Short-term Debt
Long-term Liabilities Debts due to be paid beyond one year Bonds, Long-term Loans
Shareholders' Equity Ownership stake in the company Common Stock, Retained Earnings

Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks the movement of cash in and out of a company. It helps determine whether a company has enough cash to pay its obligations and fund expansion.

Three Main Categories

1. Operating Activities

Cash from day-to-day business operations. Includes net income adjusted for depreciation, changes in working capital, etc.

2. Investing Activities

Cash used for purchasing assets and making investments. Includes capital expenditures, acquisitions, etc.

3. Financing Activities

Cash from investors and lenders. Includes debt issuance, equity financing, dividend payments, etc.

Formula: Ending Cash Balance = Beginning Cash + Operating CF + Investing CF + Financing CF

Financial Ratios

Financial ratios are useful indicators of a company's performance and financial health. They help compare companies and track progress over time.

Liquidity Ratios

Ratio Formula What It Measures
Current Ratio Current Assets / Current Liabilities Ability to pay short-term obligations
Quick Ratio (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities Ability to pay without selling inventory

Profitability Ratios

Ratio Formula What It Measures
ROA (Return on Assets) Net Income / Total Assets How efficiently assets generate profit
ROE (Return on Equity) Net Income / Shareholders' Equity Return generated for shareholders

Leverage Ratios

Ratio Formula What It Measures
Debt-to-Equity Total Liabilities / Shareholders' Equity Financial leverage and risk
Debt Ratio Total Liabilities / Total Assets Proportion of assets financed by debt
